Mechanical Hand Gear Train

About the Mechanism

This Mechanism is intended to be a functional prosthetic hand that focuses on the motion of the thumb and forefinger.


The movement of the thumb and forefinger is to be enabled through a gear train of multiple gears meshing together and connected to a motor.

The motor will provide rotational motion, which will be transferred to the finger and thumb through the gears.

The goal of this prosthetic hand is to convert an input speed of 936 RPM from the motor into a resulting rotational speed of 20 RPM at the fingers.


There are two major constraints:

    Size of chassis

    The chassis for the gear train as well as the gears must fit inside the given shell for the prosthetic hand.

    Gear Limitation

    The size of all the gears can not be less than 12 teeth and the gear ratio between two meshed gears cannot exceed 5 or be less than 1/5.

Preliminary Gear Train Design

Before performing any gear related calculations a general gear design needed to be chosen

Design 1

Consists of spur gears and worm gears which will convey the speed from the previous set of meshed gears to the next.

The primary concern for this design was whether the large middle gear would hit the top of the prosthetic hand shell.

Design 2

This gear train takes advantage of using the spur gears along a horizontal plane which would provide more room to expand the middle gear and therefore reduce the RPM from the motor to the fingers by a large margin.

The primary concern for this design was how to accomodate and calculate the speed as a result of the worm gears and whether the horizontal spur gears would interfere with any of the vertical spur gears.

Following these designs, the consensus decided to create a design similar to Design 1 but remove the worm gears as they can easily be replaced by an axle and do not affect the RPM in any manner

Final Chassis Design

As seen above the chassis design was created to match the axle alignment points while also creating minimal space between the gears and the chassis wall.

This way the chassis and gear train would be able to fit inside the prosthetic hand shell and meet one of our primary constraints.

There are also supporting walls between each system of meshed gears so the axle connections are more robust and sturdy. This also prevents any weakness in the chassis structure.

Problems and Future Solutions:

As seen above the gear train and chassis had been printed successfully but the final product had two major flaws.

1. Cuts in Chassis

As is seen above the chassis has been cut to accomodate for the hardware required to keep the gears in place (i.e. bolts to fasten the gear on the axle from either side).

As a result for not acommodating space for the bolts in the chassis design there was enough space for the gears but not enough space for the gears and the bolts. Resulting in the chassis being cut and significantly reducing the stability and structure of the chassis

Possible Solution: Accomodate hardware space into design or build a chassis with excess space and then trim down to fit.

2. Gears unable to spin axle connected gears

It is not visible in the images above but the gears on the chassis were unable to spin the gears with axle connections. Meaning the gears that were meshed were able to spin and move each other but not spin the axle.

This maybe a result of excessive drilling for the center hole of the chassis or poor adhesion of the gear to the axle.

Possible Solution: Use tiny amounts of glue to fasten the bolts to the gear firmly or if possible print the center of the gear to be a very snug fit on the axle such that minor adjustment is not needed.
